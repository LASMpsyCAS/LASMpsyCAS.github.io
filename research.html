Research

How the brain understands speech and enjoys music

Speech and music are two meaningful auditory systems: speech carries information, while music expresses emotion. LASM Lab combines psychophysics, EEG/MEG, multimodal MRI, TMS and tES to study auditory cognition across perception, development and plasticity.

01 / Speech in real life

Neural mechanisms for speech perception and comprehension

We examine how auditory, visual, motor and semantic systems cooperate when speech is degraded by noise, competing voices or incomplete cues.

  • Multisensory integration of voices, faces, lips and gestures.
  • Predictive coding mechanisms that support efficient listening.
  • Compensation and resilience during adverse listening circumstances.
02 / Development and aging

Life-span development of speech cognition

We ask how speech perception changes from development to healthy aging, and why some individuals maintain robust communication abilities across the life span.

  • Age-related changes in speech-in-noise understanding.
  • Experience-dependent protection and compensation.
  • Links among cognition, sensorimotor systems and auditory plasticity.
03 / Music, emotion and reward

Neural mechanisms of musical structure and affect

Music unfolds over time through rhythm, melody, harmony and performance cues. We study how the brain predicts musical structure and transforms sound into emotion and reward.

  • Beat, rhythm and hierarchical musical structure processing.
  • Emotion, reward, chills and autonomic responses to music.
  • Predictive coding accounts of mode, melody and musical expectation.
04 / Training and plasticity

Music training experience-related brain plasticity

Musical practice provides a powerful model of long-term auditory, motor and cognitive training. We study how training reshapes brain networks and supports communication.

  • Training-related changes in white matter, cortical activity and networks.
  • Transfer from musical expertise to speech-in-noise perception.
  • Plasticity across musicians, non-musicians and clinical-relevant contexts.
05 / Brain organization

Organizational principles for language functions and beyond

We investigate how distributed brain systems are organized for language, semantic cognition and flexible behavior, connecting local function with large-scale network topology.

  • Functional networks supporting language and semantic control.
  • Brain topography, lateralization and dynamic state transitions.
  • Computational and ontology-guided approaches to cognition.

Our work is supported by multiple agencies including the National Key Research and Development Program of China, the National Natural Science Fund for Outstanding Young Scholars and the Strategic Priority Research Program of Chinese Academy of Sciences.
